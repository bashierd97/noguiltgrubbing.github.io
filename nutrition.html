Macronutrients: the big three

Carbohydrates

Primary energy source. Choose complex carbs (whole grains, fruit, vegetables) for steady energy and digestion-friendly fiber; limit refined sugars for fewer spikes.

Proteins

Builds/repairs tissues and supports immunity. Mix lean meats, fish, eggs, tofu, beans, lentils, and nuts to cover amino acids and improve fullness.

Fats

Support hormones, cell health, and vitamin absorption. Favor unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds); minimize trans fats and excess saturated fats.

Bringing all three to each meal = steadier energy, better recovery, and easier consistency.

Micronutrients: vitamins & minerals

Small amounts, significant roles. A colorful, varied diet covers most needs — the key is eating across the full spectrum rather than relying on a handful of foods.

Key vitamins

  • Vitamin C — immune support, collagen synthesis (citrus, bell peppers, broccoli, strawberries)
  • Vitamin D — bone health, immune function (sunlight, fatty fish, fortified dairy/plant milk)
  • Vitamin A — vision, skin, immune health (sweet potato, carrots, spinach, eggs)
  • B12 — nerve function, red blood cell production (meat, eggs, dairy; fortified foods for plant-based diets)
  • Folate (B9) — cell repair and growth (lentils, spinach, avocado, edamame)

Key minerals

  • Calcium — bone density, muscle contractions (dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, almonds)
  • Iron — oxygen transport in blood (red meat, beans, lentils, spinach — pair plant sources with vitamin C for better absorption)
  • Magnesium — sleep quality, muscle recovery (nuts, seeds, dark chocolate, whole grains)
  • Potassium — blood pressure regulation, hydration (bananas, sweet potato, avocado, beans)
  • Zinc — immune function, wound healing (meat, shellfish, pumpkin seeds, chickpeas)

Eat the rainbow

Color = micronutrients. Aim for 5+ different colors across the week:

  • 🔴 Red — lycopene, vitamin C (tomato, red pepper, strawberry, watermelon)
  • 🟠 Orange — beta-carotene (sweet potato, carrot, mango, pumpkin)
  • 🟡 Yellow — lutein (corn, squash, banana, yellow pepper)
  • 🟢 Green — folate, iron, chlorophyll (spinach, broccoli, kale, peas)
  • 🫐 Blue/Purple — anthocyanins (blueberries, beets, eggplant, purple cabbage)
  • ⬜ White/Brown — allicin, fiber (garlic, onion, mushroom, cauliflower)

Hydration

Water supports every system in your body — digestion, temperature regulation, cognition, and joint lubrication. Most people are mildly dehydrated without realizing it.

How much do you need?

  • Baseline: ~8–10 cups (2–2.5L) per day for most adults
  • Active days: Add ~500ml per hour of moderate exercise
  • Hot weather: Add 1–2 extra cups
  • Pregnant/breastfeeding: Add 2–3 extra cups
  • Best check: Light yellow urine = well hydrated. Dark = drink more. Clear = possibly over-hydrated.

Signs you need more water

  • Afternoon energy slump with no clear cause
  • Headaches, especially mid-morning
  • Difficulty concentrating or brain fog
  • Hunger shortly after eating a full meal
  • Dry mouth, lips, or skin
  • Feeling irritable or anxious

Thirst lags behind actual dehydration — don't wait to feel thirsty.

Practical hydration habits

  • Drink a full glass of water first thing in the morning — you wake up dehydrated after 7–8 hours
  • Keep a water bottle on your desk in plain sight (out of sight = out of mind)
  • Add electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) after heavy sweating — plain water alone won't rehydrate you as effectively
  • Herbal tea, sparkling water, and water-rich foods (cucumber, watermelon, celery) all count toward intake
  • Drink a glass before each meal — it also helps with portion awareness

The balanced plate method

Instead of tracking every macro, use this visual framework. It works for most people, most of the time, without any counting.

½ plate — vegetables & fruit

Non-starchy vegetables and some fruit. This one habit alone covers most fiber, vitamin, and mineral targets without overthinking portions.

  • Leafy greens, broccoli, peppers, carrots, zucchini
  • Tomatoes, cucumber, mushrooms, onion, asparagus
  • Fresh or frozen — both are equally nutritious

¼ plate — quality protein

A palm-sized serving at every meal. Protein supports muscle, keeps you full for longer, and stabilizes blood sugar between meals.

  • Chicken, fish, turkey, lean beef, eggs
  • Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, low-fat dairy
  • Tofu, tempeh, edamame, lentils, chickpeas, beans

¼ plate — smart carbs + fats

Choose complex, fiber-rich carbs paired with a small amount of healthy fat to slow digestion and sustain energy across the day.

  • Brown rice, quinoa, sweet potato, oats, whole-grain bread
  • Avocado, olive oil, nuts, seeds
  • Limit refined carbs and fried foods — they spike blood sugar without satiety
